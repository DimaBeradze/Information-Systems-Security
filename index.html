üë£ Footprinting

‚ñ∂ What is Footprinting?

Footprinting is the process of collecting data that reveals system vulnerabilities which can be exploited.

üí° Key Fact: Most hackers spend 90% of their time in footprinting and only 10% performing the actual attack!

  • Essential skill for ethical hackers
  • Looking for ANY information about the target, no matter how small
  • Reveals attack vectors and weak points

‚ñ∂ Types of Footprinting

üéØ Active Footprinting

Directly extracting information from the target's website or systems. This involves direct interaction with the target.

üïµÔ∏è Passive Footprinting

No direct contact with the target. Information is gathered from third-party sources like Google, Yahoo, and public records.

‚ñ∂ Information That Can Be Gathered

  • üìç Domain names and IP addresses
  • üìû Phone numbers and contact addresses
  • üîê Access control mechanisms
  • üíª Operating system information
  • üåê Network blocks and topology
  • ‚öôÔ∏è Network applications and services

üìö Key Security Definitions

‚ñ∂ Asset

An asset is anything that needs to be protected.

Information Assets

Medical records, social security numbers, banking data, intellectual property

System Assets

Defense systems, critical infrastructure, servers, workstations

Service Assets

Websites, life/safety systems, communication platforms

Physical Assets

Facilities that house any of the above assets

‚ñ∂ Threat & Threat Agent

A threat is any potential violation of security that could cause harm to an asset.

  • Someone wanting to do harm
  • An insecure service
  • Unacknowledged systems or services

A threat agent is anyone or anything that wants to do harm or harms an asset:

  • Hackers and hacktivists
  • Malicious insiders
  • Non-malicious entities (e.g., accidental damage)

‚ñ∂ Vulnerability

A vulnerability is a flaw or weakness in the design or implementation of an asset that could be exploited.

  • ‚ùå Incorrect system configurations
  • üîì Open ports on networked computers
  • üíæ Poor backup strategies
  • üêõ Poor coding practices

‚ñ∂ Exploit

An exploit is any software or tool intentionally used to take advantage of a vulnerability.

  • üîß Hacking tools (Metasploit, Ophcrack)
  • üî® Physical tools (crowbar on a weak door)
  • üìú Scripts and malware

‚ñ∂ Risk

A risk is the probability that a threat will take advantage of a vulnerability and cause harm.

Risk Examples:

  • Backup Risk: Having only 1 backup increases risk of data loss
  • Value-based Risk: Banking systems have higher risk than personal photo collections due to their value to attackers

‚ñ∂ Attack

An attack is any intentional or unintentional event that harms or intends to harm an asset.

  • üö´ Denial of Service (DoS) attacks
  • üìä Data breaches
  • üí• Physical destruction of equipment
  • üîê Unauthorized access attempts

‚ñ∂ Mitigation & Compensating Controls

Mitigation: Any tool, service, or system that lessens the risk of attack.

Compensating Control: Any tool, service, or system that lowers risk by intentionally getting in the way of the threat.

Example: A firewall between a vulnerable system and the internet acts as a compensating control.

‚ñ∂ Understanding the CIA Triad

üîê Confidentiality

Ensuring information is accessible only to authorized individuals. Protects against unauthorized disclosure.

‚úÖ Integrity

Maintaining the accuracy and completeness of data. Ensures information hasn't been tampered with.

üöÄ Availability

Ensuring authorized users have reliable access to information and resources when needed.

üé© Types of Hackers

‚ñ∂ Black Hat Hackers

Black hat hackers have extensive knowledge about breaking into computer networks and bypassing security protocols.

  • Write malware to gain system access
  • Motivated by financial gain or malicious intent
  • Operate illegally
  • Exploit vulnerabilities for personal benefit

‚ñ∂ White Hat Hackers

White hat hackers use their powers for good rather than evil. Also known as "ethical hackers."

  • Often paid employees or contractors
  • Work as security specialists
  • Find security holes through authorized testing
  • Help organizations improve their security

‚ñ∂ Grey Hat Hackers

Grey hat hackers are a blend of both black hat and white hat activities.

  • Look for vulnerabilities without permission
  • Report findings to owners
  • Sometimes request fees to fix issues
  • Operate in ethical grey areas

‚öôÔ∏è Practical Computer Security

‚ñ∂ What is Practical Security?

Practical computer security means applying security in everyday settings effectively.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfalls:

  • Over-implementing controls can force users to find workarounds
  • Too much management overhead
  • Security that prevents legitimate work

‚ñ∂ Case Study: NAC Implementation

Initial Implementation (2007)

  • Very strict "black and white" rules
  • Required antivirus/antispyware to be fully updated
  • Windows must be completely patched
  • Users kicked off network for non-compliance

The Problem:

  • Users placed in quarantine VLAN
  • 15+ minutes to get back on network
  • 45% adoption rate
  • Users found workarounds using VPN

The Solution:

Switched vendors and added a 3-day grace period ‚Üí 95% adoption!
